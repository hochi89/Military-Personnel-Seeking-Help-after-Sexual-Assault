01   Background

Why This Study?

Sexual assault within the military is a serious and ongoing public health concern. Despite a growing research base on military mental health, the specific experiences of personnel seeking care following sexual assault remain largely unexplored.

02   Literature Review

Barriers to Care: A Multi-Level Problem

Barriers to mental health care in military populations operate simultaneously at individual, interpersonal, and institutional levels. For sexual assault survivors, these layers compound in distinct ways.

Individual Level

Stigma, shame, internalized norms of self-reliance, masculine identity, perception of help-seeking as professional failure

Interpersonal Level

Peer judgment, fear of disbelief or retaliation, command culture as both resource and deterrent

Institutional Level

Career consequences, limited gender-sensitive care, logistical obstacles, transition gaps into civilian systems

Institutional Betrayal

Survivors seeking care from the very institution associated with the assault — a compounding source of distrust

Key Findings from Prior Research

Hinton et al. (2023) found veterans rarely entered treatment proactively — most reached acute crisis first. Siegel et al. (2018) identified four avoidance clusters: autarky, ineffectiveness, heteronomy, and incapability. Karras et al. (2021) documented a boomerang effect when campaigns were perceived as part of the system that failed survivors.

The Gap This Study Addresses

Existing research documents barriers broadly but does not center first-person experiences of sexual assault survivors navigating care. No published IPA study has explored this population's subjective meaning-making of the help-seeking process.

03   Methodology

Study Design & Analytic Approach

Interpretive Phenomenological Analysis (IPA)

IPA centers participants' own meaning-making of significant lived experiences — methodologically appropriate and ethically aligned with a population historically studied about rather than with.

Research Questions

  • 1
    What are the experiences of military personnel seeking mental health support in the aftermath of sexual assault?
  • 2
    What types of mental health services do military personnel have access to following sexual assault?
  • 3
    What barriers prevent military personnel from accessing mental health support in this context?

Sampling & Recruitment

Anonymous, voluntary self-selection via flyers at VA hospitals and clinics with QR code access — reducing identification barriers for hesitant participants.

Data Collection

One semi-structured, in-depth individual interview per participant (45–90 min), audio-recorded and transcribed. Format allows consistency while giving participants narrative direction.

IPA Analysis Steps

1
Immersive reading — transcript read while listening to audio; initial impressions bracketed
2
Initial noting — detailed descriptive, linguistic, and conceptual annotations
3
Emergent themes — developed from detailed notes, recorded chronologically
4
Connections across themes — identifying relationships and clusters within each case
5
Cross-case patterns — steps 1–4 repeated; final stage identifies convergences and divergences
6
Member checking — participants consulted on emerging codes throughout analysis

04   Implications

Why This Matters

Findings are designed to produce actionable knowledge across three domains:

For Counselors

  • Trauma-informed, survivor-centered frameworks for engagement
  • Understanding how prior institutional failures shape therapeutic alliance
  • Military cultural competency as prerequisite, not supplement

For Policy & Programs

  • Evidence base for gender-sensitive care within VA and DoD systems
  • Identifying transition vulnerability windows when survivors lose familiar services
  • Accountability framework for institutional betrayal in care settings

For Outreach Efforts

  • Campaigns must be trauma-informed and co-designed with PWLE
  • Broad messaging can backfire; targeted identity-specific messaging matters
  • Trust must be rebuilt before messaging can be effective
Researcher Positionality

The principal investigator brings over 20 years of U.S. Army service, providing direct military cultural competency and personal insight into the institutional dynamics examined. Positionality is engaged reflexively throughout.
