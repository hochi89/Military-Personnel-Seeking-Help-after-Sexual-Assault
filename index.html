Military Personnel and Help-Seeking Behaviors
in the Aftermath of Sexual Assault

Hochi Rosario Cuevas & Naomi Davis
MA in Rehabilitation Counseling  ·  University of Iowa  ·  Spring 2026
Introduction

Sexual assault within the military is a serious and ongoing public health concern affecting service members across all branches and ranks. Despite a growing research base on military mental health, the specific experiences of personnel seeking care following sexual assault remain largely unexplored in the qualitative literature.

Military culture values self-reliance, emotional toughness, and unit cohesion. These values can become powerful deterrents when a service member tries to acknowledge vulnerability and seek help. For survivors of sexual assault, this cultural dynamic is compounded by stigma, fear of retaliation, and institutional betrayal.

Background & Context
71% of military personnel with alcohol problems had not sought help (Gribble et al., 2024)
30–60% co-occurrence of mental illness & substance use among homeless veterans

"It's a sign of weakness" — how male veterans describe mental health help-seeking, reflecting norms incompatible with military identity.

Purpose of the Study

This study uses Interpretive Phenomenological Analysis to explore the lived experiences of military personnel who have sought — or attempted to seek — mental health support following sexual assault. The study centers the first-person perspective of survivors, filling a critical gap in the qualitative literature.

Literature Review: Barriers to Care

Barriers to mental health care in military populations operate simultaneously at multiple levels. For sexual assault survivors, these layers compound in distinct ways.

Individual Level

Stigma, shame, internalized self-reliance norms, perception of help-seeking as professional failure

Interpersonal Level

Peer judgment, fear of disbelief or retaliation, command culture as both resource and deterrent

Institutional Level

Career consequences, limited gender-sensitive care, logistical barriers, transition gaps into civilian systems

Institutional Betrayal

Seeking care from the very institution associated with the assault — a compounding source of distrust

Key Findings from Prior Research
  • Hinton et al. (2023) — Veterans rarely entered treatment proactively; most reached acute crisis before seeking care
  • Siegel et al. (2018) — Four avoidance belief clusters: autarky, ineffectiveness, heteronomy, and incapability
  • Karras et al. (2021) — Boomerang effect: campaigns backfired when perceived as part of the system that failed survivors
  • Silvestrini & Chen (2023) — Masculine norms and military identity remain primary predictors of help-seeking reluctance
  • Mills et al. (2023) — Transition to civilian care creates a critical vulnerability window with high dropout rates
The Gap This Study Addresses

Existing research documents barriers broadly but does not center the first-person experiences of sexual assault survivors navigating care. No published IPA study has explored this population's subjective meaning-making of the help-seeking process.

Research Questions
  • 1
    What are the experiences of military personnel seeking mental health support in the aftermath of sexual assault?
  • 2
    What types of mental health services do military personnel have access to following sexual assault?
  • 3
    What barriers prevent military personnel from accessing mental health support in this context?
Research Design
Interpretive Phenomenological Analysis (IPA)

IPA was selected because it centers participants' own meaning-making of significant lived experiences — methodologically appropriate and ethically aligned with a population historically studied about rather than with. IPA engages the researcher's interpretive process reflexively, essential given the principal investigator's own military background.

Sampling & Recruitment

Anonymous, voluntary self-selection via flyers distributed at VA hospitals and clinics, with a QR code linking to the study. This approach was chosen specifically to reduce identification barriers for participants hesitant to self-identify directly.

Data Collection
  • Format: One semi-structured, in-depth individual interview per participant
  • Duration: 45–90 minutes per interview
  • Recording: Audio-recorded and professionally transcribed
  • Rationale: Semi-structured format allows consistency while giving participants direction over their own narrative
Data Analysis
  • 1
    Immersive reading — transcript read while listening to audio; initial impressions bracketed
  • 2
    Initial noting — descriptive, linguistic, and conceptual annotations
  • 3
    Emergent themes — developed from detailed notes, recorded chronologically
  • 4
    Connections across themes — identifying relationships and clusters within each case
  • 5
    Cross-case patterns — steps 1–4 repeated; convergences and divergences identified
  • 6
    Member checking — participants consulted on emerging codes throughout analysis
Implications
For Clinical Practice
  • Trauma-informed, survivor-centered frameworks for initial engagement
  • Understanding how prior institutional failures shape therapeutic alliance
  • Military cultural competency as prerequisite, not supplement
For Policy & Programs
  • Evidence base for gender-sensitive care within VA and DoD systems
  • Identifying transition vulnerability windows when survivors lose familiar services
  • Accountability framework for institutional betrayal in care settings
For Outreach Efforts
  • Campaigns must be trauma-informed and co-designed with people with lived experience
  • Broad messaging can backfire; targeted identity-specific messaging matters
  • Trust must be rebuilt before messaging can be effective
Limitations & Future Research
  • Small purposive sample limits generalizability; findings are transferable rather than generalizable
  • Self-selection bias — volunteers may differ meaningfully from non-participants
  • Researcher positionality as veteran and counselor-in-training requires ongoing reflexive attention
  • Future research should examine experiences across branch, gender identity, rank, and era of service
  • Longitudinal studies could explore whether help-seeking behaviors shift following transition to civilian life
